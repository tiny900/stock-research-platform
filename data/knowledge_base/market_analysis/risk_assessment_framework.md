# Stock Risk Assessment Framework

## Volatility-Based Risk Classification

### Risk Levels
| Annualized Volatility | Risk Level | Typical Stocks | Position Sizing |
|----------------------|------------|----------------|-----------------|
| < 15% | LOW | Large-cap utilities, staples | Full position |
| 15% - 25% | MEDIUM | Large-cap tech, financials | 75% position |
| 25% - 40% | HIGH | Growth stocks, mid-caps | 50% position |
| > 40% | VERY HIGH | Speculative, small-caps | 25% position |

### Maximum Drawdown Context
- S&P 500 average annual max drawdown: ~14%
- Bear market average max drawdown: ~35%
- Individual stock max drawdown can exceed 50% (even blue chips during crises)
- Recovery time from 50% drawdown requires 100% gain to break even

## Multi-Factor Risk Assessment

### 1. Market Risk
- Beta relative to S&P 500
- Correlation to market during stress periods
- Sensitivity to interest rate changes

### 2. Company-Specific Risk
- Revenue concentration (single product/customer dependency)
- Debt levels and interest coverage
- Management quality and governance
- Competitive moat sustainability

### 3. Sector Risk
- Regulatory environment changes
- Technology disruption threats
- Commodity price exposure
- Cyclicality of the business

### 4. Macro Risk
- GDP growth sensitivity
- Inflation impact on margins
- Currency exposure for multinational companies
- Geopolitical risk factors

## Combining Technical and Fundamental Risk
The most robust risk assessment combines both approaches:
- **Technical**: Current trend, volatility regime, proximity to support/resistance
- **Fundamental**: Valuation, earnings quality, balance sheet strength
- **Sentiment**: Analyst ratings, short interest, options positioning

A stock with deteriorating technical indicators AND weakening fundamentals presents the highest risk. Conversely, strong technicals supported by solid fundamentals and reasonable valuation present lower risk.
