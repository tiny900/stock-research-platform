# Fundamental Analysis Metrics

## P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)
The P/E ratio compares a company's stock price to its earnings per share (EPS). Calculated as: P/E = Stock Price / EPS. A high P/E may indicate overvaluation or high growth expectations, while a low P/E may suggest undervaluation or declining earnings. The S&P 500 historical average P/E is approximately 15-17. Forward P/E uses estimated future earnings, while trailing P/E uses past 12 months.

## EPS (Earnings Per Share)
EPS represents the portion of a company's profit allocated to each outstanding share of common stock. Basic EPS = (Net Income - Preferred Dividends) / Weighted Average Shares Outstanding. Diluted EPS accounts for convertible securities. EPS growth rate is a key metric — consistent EPS growth indicates a healthy, expanding business.

## Market Capitalization
Market cap represents the total market value of a company's outstanding shares. Calculated as: Market Cap = Share Price × Shares Outstanding. Large-cap: over $10 billion; Mid-cap: $2-10 billion; Small-cap: under $2 billion. Market cap determines index inclusion and is used for peer comparison within sectors.

## Revenue and Revenue Growth
Revenue (or sales) is the total income generated from business operations before expenses are deducted. Revenue growth rate = (Current Revenue - Prior Revenue) / Prior Revenue × 100%. Consistent revenue growth indicates strong demand for products/services. Year-over-year (YoY) comparisons eliminate seasonal effects.

## Profit Margins
Gross margin = (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue. Operating margin = Operating Income / Revenue. Net margin = Net Income / Revenue. Higher margins indicate better cost control and pricing power. Margins should be compared within the same industry. Expanding margins are generally positive; shrinking margins may signal competitive pressure.

## P/B Ratio (Price-to-Book)
P/B ratio compares market price to book value per share. Book Value = Total Assets - Total Liabilities. P/B > 1 means the market values the company above its accounting value. Technology companies often have high P/B ratios due to intangible assets. Financial institutions are commonly evaluated using P/B.

## Dividend Yield
Dividend Yield = Annual Dividends Per Share / Stock Price × 100%. It measures the cash return to shareholders relative to stock price. High yield may indicate value or distress; low yield may indicate growth focus. Dividend payout ratio = Dividends / Net Income — sustainable payouts are typically below 60-70%.

## Free Cash Flow (FCF)
FCF = Operating Cash Flow - Capital Expenditures. It represents the cash available to the company after maintaining or expanding its asset base. Positive FCF enables dividends, buybacks, debt repayment, and acquisitions. FCF yield = FCF per share / Stock Price — a metric for comparing valuations across companies.

## Debt-to-Equity Ratio
D/E Ratio = Total Debt / Total Shareholders' Equity. It measures financial leverage. A ratio above 1.0 means more debt than equity. Acceptable levels vary by industry — capital-intensive industries (utilities, real estate) typically have higher D/E ratios. Increasing D/E may indicate aggressive financing.

## Return on Equity (ROE)
ROE = Net Income / Shareholders' Equity × 100%. It measures how effectively management uses equity to generate profits. ROE above 15-20% is generally considered good. DuPont analysis decomposes ROE into: Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier, revealing the source of returns.

## PEG Ratio (Price/Earnings to Growth)
PEG = P/E Ratio / Annual EPS Growth Rate. It adjusts the P/E ratio for earnings growth. PEG = 1 is considered fairly valued; PEG < 1 may be undervalued given growth; PEG > 1 may be overvalued. PEG is especially useful for comparing high-growth companies where P/E alone can be misleading.

## Beta
Beta measures a stock's volatility relative to the overall market (S&P 500 = 1.0). Beta > 1 means more volatile than the market; Beta < 1 means less volatile. A beta of 1.5 implies the stock tends to move 1.5x the market's movement. Beta is used in the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) to calculate expected returns.
