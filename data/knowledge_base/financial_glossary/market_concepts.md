# Market Concepts and Trading Terms

## Bull Market vs Bear Market
A bull market is characterized by rising prices and optimistic investor sentiment, typically defined as a 20% or more increase from recent lows. A bear market is the opposite — a 20% or more decline from recent highs. Bull markets tend to last longer than bear markets. The average bull market lasts about 4.4 years, while the average bear market lasts about 11.3 months.

## Market Sectors
The Global Industry Classification Standard (GICS) divides the stock market into 11 sectors: Technology, Healthcare, Financials, Consumer Discretionary, Consumer Staples, Energy, Industrials, Materials, Real Estate, Utilities, and Communication Services. Sector rotation — shifting investments between sectors based on economic cycles — is a common investment strategy.

## Index Funds and ETFs
An index fund tracks a specific market index (e.g., S&P 500, NASDAQ-100). ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds) trade like stocks on exchanges. Both provide diversification at low cost. The S&P 500 represents approximately 80% of total US market capitalization. Key indices include: S&P 500, Dow Jones Industrial Average (30 large-cap stocks), and NASDAQ Composite (tech-heavy).

## Risk Management
Position sizing determines how much capital to allocate to each trade. Stop-loss orders automatically sell when price drops to a preset level. Diversification across asset classes, sectors, and geographies reduces portfolio risk. The Sharpe Ratio measures risk-adjusted returns: (Portfolio Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Portfolio Standard Deviation. A Sharpe Ratio above 1 is generally considered good.

## Order Types
Market order: buy/sell immediately at the current best price. Limit order: buy/sell only at a specified price or better. Stop order: triggers a market order when price reaches a specified level. Stop-limit order: triggers a limit order at the stop price. Trailing stop: adjusts automatically as price moves in your favor.

## Candlestick Patterns
Candlestick charts display open, high, low, and close prices. Common patterns include: Doji (indecision — open equals close), Hammer (potential bullish reversal at bottom), Shooting Star (potential bearish reversal at top), Engulfing (strong reversal signal), and Morning/Evening Star (three-candle reversal patterns). These patterns are most reliable when confirmed by volume and other indicators.

## Market Capitalization Weighting
Most major indices are market-cap weighted, meaning larger companies have more influence on index performance. This means the top 10 stocks in the S&P 500 can represent 25-35% of the total index. Equal-weight indices give each stock the same influence regardless of size, providing different exposure characteristics.

## Earnings Season
Earnings season occurs four times per year when publicly traded companies report quarterly financial results. Companies typically report 2-6 weeks after the quarter ends. Key metrics reported include revenue, EPS, and forward guidance. Earnings surprises (beating or missing analyst estimates) often cause significant stock price movements. Pre-market and after-hours trading often sees high volatility during earnings announcements.

## Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)
DCA is an investment strategy where a fixed dollar amount is invested at regular intervals regardless of price. This approach reduces the impact of volatility on the overall purchase price. Over time, DCA results in buying more shares when prices are low and fewer when prices are high. It is especially popular for retirement investing and index fund accumulation.

## Short Selling
Short selling involves borrowing shares and selling them with the expectation of buying them back at a lower price. The short seller profits from the price decline. Risks include unlimited potential losses (price can rise indefinitely), short squeezes (forced buying when price rises), and borrowing costs. Short interest (shares sold short / total float) is a metric of bearish sentiment.
